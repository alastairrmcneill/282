Climb one Munro and you'll probably want to climb another. Somewhere around the tenth, the
question sneaks in: *could I do them all?* Here's what a full round actually involves.

## The short answer

**Most compleaters take 10–25 years.** The average, according to Scottish Mountaineering
Club data, is around two decades from first Munro to last. At 12–15 Munros a year — a
solid, sustainable hobby pace — you'll finish in about 20 years. Push to 25–30 a year and
a decade will do it.

That long tail is part of the point. A Munro round is a relationship with Scotland: you'll
see the Highlands in every season, learn to read weather and maps like a local, and finish
a different (and fitter) person than the one who started.

## The records

At the other extreme, the Munros have become an endurance racecourse:

- **Fastest self-propelled round**: Jamie Aarons set the current benchmark in 2023,
  finishing all 282 in **31 days, 10 hours and 27 minutes**, travelling between hills by
  foot, bike and kayak.
- **Fastest winter round**: Martin Moran's legendary 1984–85 winter round took 83 days;
  winter continuous rounds remain among the rarest feats in British mountaineering.
- **First recorded round**: the Rev. A. E. Robertson in 1901. It took another 22 years
  for someone to repeat the feat.

## What a round actually adds up to

Back-of-envelope numbers for the whole list:

- **282 summits**, many shared across ridge days — most rounds need roughly **150–180
  separate hill days**.
- Around **150,000 metres of ascent** — seventeen Everests from sea level.
- Roughly **3,000 kilometres of walking**.
- One rock climb — the [Inaccessible Pinnacle](/munros/inaccessible-pinnacle) — and a
  handful of serious scrambles (see [the hardest Munros](/guides/hardest-munros)).

## Planning your round

**Don't save the hard ones for last by accident.** Skye's Cuillin demands dry rock and
nerve; do it mid-round while your scrambling is sharp, not as a dreaded finale — unless
you want the In Pinn as your triumphant finish, which is a popular choice too.

**Use multi-Munro days.** Ridges like South Glen Shiel (seven Munros), the Mamores (up to
ten for the strong) and the Fannichs make the arithmetic friendly. The
"[commonly climbed with](/munros)" section on every Munro page shows which peaks pair
naturally.

**Watch the remote pockets.** Fisherfield, Knoydart and Mull each need a full weekend or
more. Plan them as trips, not day raids.

**Keep the log honest.** Decades are long; memories blur. Log every summit with its date
— in the [282 app](/) each tick is stamped, mapped and backed up, and you can set an
annual challenge to keep the momentum going. When you finally stand on number 282, you'll
have the whole story in your pocket.

## Becoming a compleater

When you finish, you can report your round to the **Scottish Mountaineering Club** and
join the register of Munroists that stretches back to 1901 — over 7,000 names and
counting. Tradition calls for champagne on the final summit and, ideally, a crowd.

Then, of course, come the questions: the Tops? The Corbetts? A second round? (Plenty of
people are on their third.)
