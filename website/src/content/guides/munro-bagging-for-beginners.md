So you've decided to start Munro bagging — welcome to a hobby that will take you to the
wildest corners of Scotland and, if it really gets its hooks into you, occupy the next
decade or two of your weekends. Here's everything you need to get going.

## Step 1: Understand what you're taking on

A Munro is a Scottish mountain over 3,000 feet — there are
[282 of them](/guides/what-is-a-munro). They range from gentle half-day strolls to serious
scrambles on the Skye Cuillin. Nobody starts with the hard ones. The skill of Munro
bagging is matching the mountain to your experience, fitness and the weather.

## Step 2: Pick a good first Munro

Your first Munro should have a clear path, simple navigation and a manageable day length.
Three classics:

- **[Schiehallion](/munros/schiehallion)** — the "fairy hill of the Caledonians". A broad,
  well-made path and one of the most famous silhouettes in Scotland.
- **[Ben Lomond](/munros/ben-lomond)** — the most southerly Munro, an hour from Glasgow,
  with a motorway of a path and glorious Loch Lomond views.
- **[Ben Chonzie](/munros/ben-chonzie)** — a gentle track most of the way, famous for
  mountain hares.

Our [easiest Munros guide](/guides/easiest-munros) has ten more options.

## Step 3: Get the essential gear

You don't need to spend a fortune, but some things are non-negotiable, even in August:

- **Boots** with ankle support and a decent tread — paths are rocky, wet and boggy.
- **Waterproof jacket and trousers** — Scottish weather changes in minutes.
- **Warm layers** (fleece or synthetic, not cotton), plus **hat and gloves** year-round.
- **Map and compass** — a paper OS map of the area and the ability to take a bearing.
  Phone apps are brilliant until the battery dies in the cold.
- **Food, water and a head torch** — more than you think you'll need.
- **First aid kit and an emergency shelter/bivvy bag** for the day things go wrong.

**Winter is a different sport.** Between roughly November and April, Munros carry snow and
ice, and require ice axe, crampons and the training to use them. As a beginner, build your
experience in late spring, summer and early autumn.

## Step 4: Learn the safety basics

- **Check the mountain forecast** the night before and the morning of your walk — MWIS and
  the Met Office both publish dedicated mountain-weather forecasts. The 282 app shows a
  7-day forecast for every Munro to help you pick the right day.
- **Tell someone** where you're going and when you'll be back.
- **Know your escape routes** and be genuinely willing to turn back. The summit will still
  be there next weekend.
- **Daylight** — in December Scotland gets barely seven hours; plan short days in winter.
- **Emergency**: call 999, ask for Police then Mountain Rescue. Signal is patchy —
  register with Emergency SMS before you need it.

## Step 5: Log your progress

Half the joy of Munro bagging is the list itself — watching the ticks accumulate over
years. Traditionally that meant pencil marks in the back of a guidebook. The
[282 app](/) gives you the modern version: every Munro on an interactive map, your
completions logged with dates and photos, badges for milestones, and a feed of what your
friends are climbing. It's free on [iOS](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/282/id6474512889)
and [Android](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.alastairrmcneill.TwoEightTwo).

## Step 6: Build up gradually

After a few easy Munros, the natural progression is:

1. **Multi-Munro days** — many Munros come in natural pairs and groups sharing a ridge,
   like the seven of the South Glen Shiel Ridge.
2. **Bigger, remoter days** — the Cairngorm giants like
   [Ben Macdui](/munros/ben-macdui) and [Braeriach](/munros/braeriach).
3. **Scrambling** — hands-on ridges like Aonach Eagach or An Teallach, and eventually the
   [hardest Munros](/guides/hardest-munros) on Skye.

Take it at your own pace. The mountains aren't going anywhere — and there are
[282 of them](/munros) waiting.
