Most of the 282 Munros are walks — long ones, steep ones, boggy ones, but walks. A handful
are something else entirely. These are the Munros that baggers save for last, train for,
and talk about for years afterwards.

## The Inaccessible Pinnacle — the hardest Munro of all

The [Inaccessible Pinnacle](/munros/inaccessible-pinnacle) (986m) is a blade of gabbro
jutting from the ridge of Sgurr Dearg on Skye, and the only Munro that requires **actual
rock climbing**. The standard route up the east ridge is graded Moderate — easy by
climbing standards, unthinkable by walking ones — with what the classic description calls
"a drop on one side and an even bigger one on the other". The descent is by abseil.

Sir Hugh Munro himself never climbed it, which is why tradition treats the In Pinn as the
crux of every round. Most baggers hire a mountain guide for the day — worth every penny.

## The rest of the Skye Cuillin

The Black Cuillin is Britain's most alpine ridge, and its eleven other Munros make up most
of the "hard list":

- **[Sgurr Alasdair](/munros/sgurr-alasdair)** (992m) — the highest peak on Skye, reached
  via the Great Stone Chute or scrambling lines.
- **[Sgurr Mhic Choinnich](/munros/sgurr-mhic-choinnich)** (948m) — exposed ledges and
  the famous "Collie's Ledge" traverse.
- **[Am Basteir](/munros/am-basteir)** (934m) — the "executioner", with its awkward "bad
  step".
- **[Sgurr nan Gillean](/munros/sgurr-nan-gillean)** (964m) — the classic pointed summit,
  a sustained scramble by its easiest route.
- **[Bruach na Frithe](/munros/bruach-na-frithe)** (958m) — the "easy" one, and the best
  first taste of the Cuillin.

Bare rock, magnetic gabbro that makes compasses lie, and sea-level starts: Skye days are
long, technical and utterly unforgettable.

## Mainland scrambles

- **Aonach Eagach, Glen Coe** — the narrowest ridge on the mainland links
  [Meall Dearg](/munros/meall-dearg-aonach-eagach) and
  [Sgorr nam Fiannaidh](/munros/sgorr-nam-fiannaidh-aonach-eagach). Once you start the pinnacled
  section there's no easy way off.
- **An Teallach** — the great Torridonian castle of the northwest. Its two Munros,
  [Bidein a' Ghlas Thuill](/munros/bidein-a-ghlas-thuill-an-teallach) and
  [Sgurr Fiona](/munros/sgurr-fiona-an-teallach), can be reached by walkers' paths, but the full
  traverse over the Corrag Bhuidhe pinnacles is one of Britain's finest scrambles.
- **Liathach** — Torridon's wall of a mountain: two Munros linked by the exposed Am
  Fasarinen pinnacles (with a path that sneaks below for the sensible).
- **The Saddle, Glen Shiel** — best climbed by the Forcan Ridge, a brilliant, airy
  scramble that's entirely optional but shouldn't be.

## Hard for other reasons

Difficulty isn't only about scrambling:

- **The Fisherfield Six** (five Munros since the 2011 re-survey) — the remotest group in
  Scotland. Most parties backpack in and camp; [A' Mhaighdean](/munros/a-mhaighdean) is
  often called the most remote Munro of all.
- **[Ben More](/munros/ben-more-mull) on Mull and [Ben Hope](/munros/ben-hope)** in the
  far north — not technical, but the logistics (a ferry, a very long drive) mean they
  guard many people's final tick.
- **[Ben Macdui](/munros/ben-macdui) and the Cairngorm plateau** — easy angles,
  deadly-serious navigation in mist or winter storms.

## How to prepare

1. **Build up gradually** — get mileage on ordinary Munros first (start with our
   [beginner's guide](/guides/munro-bagging-for-beginners)).
2. **Learn to scramble** — practise on graded scrambles below 3,000ft before committing
   to Aonach Eagach or An Teallach.
3. **Pick your weather ruthlessly** — dry rock and light winds transform these days.
4. **Consider a guide for Skye** — many baggers do the whole Cuillin in 2–3 guided days
   and rate them the best of their round.
