A **Munro is a Scottish mountain over 3,000 feet (914.4 metres)** in height. There are
currently **282 of them**, and climbing them all — "Munro bagging" — is one of the most
popular long-term challenges in British hillwalking.

## Where does the name come from?

The name comes from **Sir Hugh Munro**, a founding member of the Scottish Mountaineering
Club (SMC). In 1891 he published his famous *Munro's Tables*, the first serious attempt to
catalogue every Scottish summit over 3,000 feet. Before his survey, nobody was sure how many
there were — guesses ranged from thirty to over three hundred.

Ironically, Sir Hugh never completed his own list. He died in 1919 with a handful of summits
unclimbed, including the [Inaccessible Pinnacle](/munros/inaccessible-pinnacle) on Skye — by
common consent the hardest Munro of them all.

## How many Munros are there?

**282.** The number isn't fixed forever, though. The SMC maintains the official list, and
modern surveying occasionally moves a mountain across the 3,000ft line. Recent decades have
seen several changes — [Beinn Teallach](/munros/beinn-teallach) was promoted in 1984, while
Sgurr nan Ceannaichean was demoted in 2009 after being re-measured at 2,997ft.

The full range runs from [Ben Nevis](/munros/ben-nevis) at 1,345m — the highest mountain in
the British Isles — down to [Beinn Teallach](/munros/beinn-teallach) at 915m, just two metres
over the line. You can browse [the complete list of all 282 Munros](/munros), organised by
region.

## Munros vs Munro Tops, Corbetts and Grahams

- **Munro** — a distinct Scottish mountain over 3,000ft. There are 282.
- **Munro Top** — a subsidiary summit over 3,000ft that isn't considered a separate
  mountain (around 226 of them).
- **Corbett** — a Scottish hill between 2,500ft and 3,000ft with at least 500ft of drop on
  all sides (222 of them).
- **Graham** — a Scottish hill between 2,000ft and 2,500ft (219 of them).

Whether a 3,000ft summit counts as a full Munro or just a Top is a matter of judgement —
the SMC weighs distance, drop and character. That ambiguity is part of the list's charm,
and arguments about it have been running for 130 years.

## What is Munro bagging?

Munro bagging is simply the game of climbing them all. Reach a summit and you've "bagged"
it. Finish all 282 and you become a **Munroist** (or "compleater", with the traditional
archaic spelling) — your name can be recorded in the SMC's official register, which now
lists more than 7,000 people.

A round of the Munros takes most people **10–25 years** of weekends and holidays, though
some compress it dramatically — see
[how long it takes to climb all 282 Munros](/guides/climbing-all-the-munros).

There's no committee checking your claims: Munro bagging runs on honesty. But most baggers
keep a log — a notebook, a spreadsheet, or an app like [282](/) that tracks your progress
on a map, remembers your summit dates and photos, and shows you exactly which peaks are
still waiting.
